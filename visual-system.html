FreightSimple / Visual System · Brand Foundation
A working document · v1.0

The visual system that supports how we sound.

This document describes the visual system used across FreightSimple's product, marketing, and internal communication. It exists so the system can be defended, taught, extended, and challenged with the same rigor it was built with. Every choice here serves a function. None of it is decoration.

01 · Opening principle

What we are building visually, and why.

Visual systems exist to do one job: make the brand legible to its real audience, in the contexts they actually encounter it. Not to look beautiful in isolation. Not to follow industry conventions. Not to impress designers. To support the conversation we're trying to have with shippers.

So before describing what the system looks like, we describe two things: the audience we're designing for, and the visual landscape they currently navigate. The system makes sense only against those two backdrops.

Visual choices are strategic choices. A system that looks like every other broker tells the customer we are like every other broker. A system that looks different earns a second of attention — and a second is everything.

— The premise

03 · The category reality

What the rest of the category looks like.

Open ten Canadian LTL freight broker websites. The visual pattern is consistent enough to feel coordinated:

Brand-blue logos rendered large over white backgrounds. Stock photos of semi-trucks against sunsets. Maps with pins. Hero CTAs that say "Get a Quote Now." Stat badges with checkmarks. Photos of warehouse workers giving thumbs-up. The same five-or-six template moves, the same restrained-but-trying-to-be-trustworthy aesthetic.

This is not a critique of those brands. It's a description of a saturated category. When everyone looks the same, no one is remembered. A buyer comparing five brokers visits five sites that visually blur together — and then chooses the one with the cheapest quote, because nothing else differentiated them.

If we look like every other broker, we compete on price alone. If we look different — distinctive, considered, sophisticated — we earn a second of attention and a chance to compete on substance.

04 · The system, named

The four principles the system rests on.

Every visual decision in this system traces back to one of these four. If a future addition can't be explained by one, it probably doesn't belong.

Principle 01

Editorial over promotional.

The system reads like a publication, not an ad. Generous space, considered typography, deliberate hierarchy. Built to be read, not skimmed.

Principle 02

Restraint earns attention.

One bold gesture per surface. Not three. The discipline of subtraction signals confidence — and gives the eye a place to land.

Principle 03

Color carries meaning.

Each color in the system has a job. Dark for ground. Cream for warmth. Gold for accent. Blue for action. Used precisely, not decoratively.

Principle 04

Type does heavy lifting.

Typography sets the tone before any image does. We pair a strong display face with a refined body face, and let the contrast do the work.

05 · The blue, specifically

How the logo blue lives in the system.

The logo blue (#4C62EA) is the single brand color most commonly misunderstood — by stakeholders, by future designers, and sometimes by ourselves. It deserves its own section.

The blue is not subordinated. It is concentrated.

In the visual system, the blue does not dominate large surfaces. It does not sit behind hero copy. It does not paint navigation bars or footers. That is a deliberate decision, not a deprioritization.

Bright blue, used in volume across a brand system, does two things — both unhelpful. First, it saturates the eye, making everything feel "branded" in a generic way. Second, it makes the brand feel like it's trying. Both effects work against the sophisticated, considered tone the rest of the system is building.

Where the blue actually lives

The blue concentrates in the moments where it has the most semantic weight: action and interaction.

  • Primary CTAs and buttons
  • Links and interactive text
  • Active states in navigation
  • Section labels and accent strokes (in the dashboard, this manual)
  • Form focus states and validation
  • Brand callouts where attention is the point

This concentration increases the value of the blue rather than dilutes it. When the user sees blue, they know something. It signals "this is where to act." That signaling power is destroyed if blue is also the background, the header, the footer, the everything.

The brand color is more powerful when it means something specific than when it's everywhere. Used with restraint, the logo blue becomes a signal. Used everywhere, it becomes wallpaper.

— On color discipline

The relationship between the blue and the dark/cream system

Think of it this way: dark and cream are the room. Gold is the architecture. Blue is the doorway.

The room (dark + cream) sets the tone — sophisticated, warm, considered. The architecture (gold) creates structure and emphasis. The doorway (blue) is what the visitor walks through to take action. Each element does its job. None of them tries to be the others.

06 · The system in action

How this shows up in real outputs.

Three examples from current FreightSimple work. Each one shows the principles applied — and the blue used precisely.

Marketing Operations Dashboard

Dark hero, gold accents, blue for action.

The dashboard's hero section uses the dark surface to convey strategic gravitas. Gold accents mark key metrics and decision moments. The blue appears in CTAs, section labels, and the active state of the Q2/Q3 toggle — the points where the reader is invited to interact. The blue is highly visible without being dominant.

Retention Email Sequence

Cream + dark + gold for warmth, blue for the click.

The email templates use a warm cream background with dark brand strips and gold accents in data labels. The CTA buttons use dark with gold text, with the blue reserved for embedded text links and the action moments. The system is doing its job: the CTA pulls the eye not because of color volume, but because of contrast and placement.

Internal Documents (Brand Book, Handbook)

Editorial restraint, with the blue as section-marker.

Internal long-form documents use Inter for body and Plus Jakarta Sans for headers, with cream and white surfaces. The blue identifies section labels and key cross-references — turning navigation into a visual cue. The result reads like a thoughtful publication, not a corporate one-pager.

07 · What this is not

What the system isn't — to protect it from misuse.

A system without explicit limits gets eroded over time. These are the misunderstandings that would, if accepted, dissolve the system into something less coherent.

×

This is not "moving away from the brand blue."

The blue is brand-central and remains so. It lives where action lives. Anyone reading the system as "the blue was demoted" has misread the intent — and the actual deliverables, where the blue is consistently present.

×

This is not a tech-company aesthetic borrowed wholesale.

The system shares vocabulary with sophisticated tech brands (restrained color, type-led hierarchy, generous space) because those choices solve the same problems we have: an audience that's tool-fluent and time-poor. We borrow patterns where they work for our audience, not because the patterns are fashionable.

×

This is not a fixed system.

The system is a working document. As the brand learns more about its audience, channels, and context, the system evolves. Changes should be debated against the four principles in section 04 — additions that don't ladder up to one of those principles probably shouldn't ship.

08 · Maintained by

Who owns this, and when we revisit.

The visual system is owned by Marketing and lives alongside the Brand Book v1.0 in the team handbook. Updates require a documented reason tied to one of the four principles.

Review cadence: end of each quarter, alongside the broader brand audit. Specifically: are the principles still the right ones? Are real outputs respecting them? Has the audience or category shifted in a way that should reshape the system?

How to use this document: when starting any new visual work — campaign, page, asset, internal doc — open this first. Find the section that maps to your decision (palette, type, blue usage). If your decision can't be defended through one of the principles, pause and reconsider.

The point of writing this down is simple: a system that exists in someone's head dies when that head leaves. A system that's documented can be taught, defended, extended, and challenged. This document is the system's insurance policy against being forgotten — and against being changed for the wrong reasons.

If anything in here doesn't match what you're seeing in the real outputs, that's worth flagging. The system should be a description of what we actually do, not a description of what we wish we did. Discrepancies are the most useful kind of feedback.

— Daniela Perea, Marketing Lead
