/02Strategy · what paid IS and ISN'T for us

For FreightSimple at this stage, paid is performance, not awareness.

Google Search Ads sit at bottom-funnel by definition. Someone typing "LTL freight quote Canada" is already in market, already comparing. We're not building awareness with that spend; we're capturing intent that already exists. Framing paid this way makes every other downstream decision clearer.

Paid in the funnel, where each channel actually plays

Channels behave differently. Treating them as interchangeable is the most expensive mistake in paid. Here's how we position each one for Q2:

⚐ Bottom-funnel
Google Search · Bing
Search intent. People already looking. Optimize hard for conversion. Measure quote-to-spend.
⚐ Mid-funnel
LinkedIn Ads
Audience-targeted, not intent-targeted. Right people, before they've decided to compare. Measure assisted conversions and engagement.
⚐ Top-funnel
Retargeting · Display
Bring back visitors who didn't convert. Lower CPM, secondary touchpoints. Measure return visits and assisted conversions.

★ The principle that holds this together

Every dollar of paid spend must have a named persona, a named funnel stage, and a named conversion event. If we can't fill those three blanks for a campaign, the campaign doesn't run. This is the same rule Strategy Base enforces for tactics, applied to paid at the campaign level.

/03Conversion · the events we'll measure

Four tiers. One primary. Clear weights.

Smart Bidding algorithms need a clear signal hierarchy. We don't have one yet, and without it, Google's algorithm optimizes to whatever it can find, which is rarely what we'd choose. This taxonomy fixes that.

The four-tier event structure

Tier 1
Quote-to-booking
event: booking_completed · value: $$
The conversion that pays the business. The dollar number actually moves the model. Eventually the optimization target.
Weight1.0
Tier 2
Quote request
event: quote_submitted
Strongest pre-purchase signal. Our optimization target for Q2, high enough volume to teach Smart Bidding.
Weight0.4
Tier 3
Account signup
event: signup_completed
Micro-conversion. Adds shape to the model but isn't a real buying signal on its own.
Weight0.1
Tier 4
Engaged sessions
event: engaged_session_3min · pricing_page_view
Awareness-adjacent. Reported for context. Never used as the primary optimization target for Search.
Weight0.02

Why Tier 2 first, not Tier 1

Google's Smart Bidding needs ~30–50 conversions per month minimum per campaign to learn properly. Below that threshold, the algorithm doesn't have enough signal and bids erratically. Quote-to-booking volume is probably below that threshold right now, we'd be starving the model.

Quote requests, by contrast, happen at roughly 5–10× the rate of bookings. Optimizing to Tier 2 gives Smart Bidding the signal density it needs. Once Tier 2 volume is stable and we have reliable quote→booking conversion rates measured (Week 6–8), we graduate to Tier 1 optimization.

↳ The threshold rule

We graduate from Tier 2 to Tier 1 optimization when: (a) any single campaign generates ≥40 bookings/month consistently, and (b) we have ≥8 weeks of quote→booking data to calibrate value. Below those thresholds, we stay on Tier 2.

What needs to happen on our side first

Conversion tracking is only as good as the events firing. Before we touch bidding strategies, we close these gaps:

01Verify all 4 events fire correctly in Google Ads and PostHog. Sofia tests every event end-to-end Week 1.
02Add booking value to the booking event payload. Without value, Google can't run value-based bidding properly.
03Connect Google Ads to PostHog and CRM. Today they're not joined, we have to manually match. Build the integration once, save hours every week.
04Mark quote_submitted and booking_completed as "primary" in Google Ads. The other two stay secondary.
05Set the lookback window to 30 days for booking, 7 days for quote, matches the actual buying journey.
/04Budget · framework for Q2

Audit first. Reallocate next. Scale last.

Scaling before auditing is the most common way to lose money in paid. We don't fall for it. The Q2 budget plan sequences three phases, and the size of the bet grows as confidence grows.

The three-phase sequence

Phase 01
Audit
Weeks 1–3 · ~$13.5K spend
  • Maintain current $150/day Google Ads
  • Map which keywords actually convert
  • Measure baseline CAC, CTR, CVR by campaign
  • Build the CRM ↔ Ads attribution link
  • Identify the bottom 30% to reallocate
Phase 02
Reallocate
Weeks 4–6 · ~$13.5K spend
  • Cut bottom 30% (negative keywords, paused campaigns)
  • Redirect freed budget to top-converting keywords
  • Launch Bing mirror campaigns (low-cost test)
  • Launch LinkedIn Ads pilot ($1.5K)
  • Expected: ~25% lift in quotes at same spend
Phase 03
Scale
Weeks 7–13 · ~$20K–$28K spend
  • Increase budget only on proven campaigns
  • LinkedIn full rollout if pilot validated
  • Bing scale-up if CPC stays favorable
  • End-of-quarter review and Q3 planning
  • Decision point: programmatic for Q3?
⚠ The discipline this protects

Each phase has a go/no-go gate. If Audit doesn't surface enough signal by Week 3, Reallocate gets a one-week extension so we move on solid data. If Reallocate doesn't lift quotes by Week 6, we recalibrate Scale against fresh signal. Phases follow data, not the calendar.

Audit framework · weeks 1–3

What we measure before we change anything

Before any structural change, we map the existing account. The audit builds the baseline against which every later decision is judged.

01Keyword-level performance. Pull all keywords with ≥30 days of data. Bucket by quote conversion rate. Top quintile, bottom quintile.
02Match-type performance. Exact vs Phrase vs Broad. In B2B niche markets, Broad often costs more without lifting quality.
03Search-term report. What people actually typed. Build the negative keyword list from here.
04Geo performance. Province-by-province CTR and CVR. Identify high-converting metros vs low-performing ones.
05Ad copy performance. Which headlines and descriptions get clicks that convert vs clicks that bounce.
06Landing page performance. Which destinations convert. Surface this back to the homepage refresh project.
07Device performance. Mobile vs desktop conversion gap. In LTL B2B, desktop usually dominates.
08Time-of-day / day-of-week. Business-hours bias for B2B is typical, but worth confirming for Canadian LTL specifically.

Pilot success criteria

When to scale, when to hold, when to kill

↑ Scale to $7.5K Phase 03

Cost per quote request ≤ $250 on any single audience after 3 weeks of pilot. CTR ≥ 0.5%. Any audience that hits the threshold gets scaled; the others get iterated on or killed.

⚠ Hold and iterate

Cost per quote request $250–$500. Engagement above baseline but conversions soft. Iterate on creative for 2 more weeks before deciding scale or kill.

Cost per quote request > $500 after 4 weeks of iteration. Pause the audience. Redirect budget to Google Search where the conversion math is proven.

/09Testing & learning agenda

Five tests. One at a time. Real significance thresholds.

Parallel A/B tests in paid are the most common way to learn nothing. Different tests interfere, traffic gets split too thin, and results land in the "directional but inconclusive" zone. We run one test at a time per channel, define significance up front, and don't pull the plug or the trigger early.

The five priority tests for Q2

1
Headline angle, "Save Money" vs "Prevent Problems" vs "Automate"
Weeks 2–4 · Google

Three message angles map directly to the three brand pillars. We don't know which one converts hardest at the ad layer. Run as Responsive Search Ads with the three angles as pinned headlines, measure quote-rate per angle.

Hypothesis · "Save Money" wins for Sandra/Michael; "Prevent Problems" wins for Alex Ops. Significance · 95%, ≥150 conversions per arm Outcome · Wins become the default for that audience.
2
Landing page, Current homepage vs persona-specific landing
Weeks 4–7 · Google + LinkedIn

Hypothesis behind the homepage refresh project: a generic homepage is the wrong destination for paid traffic. Build 2 persona-specific landings (Sandra and Michael first), split 50/50 with current homepage, measure bounce and quote rate.

Hypothesis · Persona-specific landings lift quote rate ≥ 20%. Significance · 95%, ≥200 conversions per arm Outcome · Feeds the homepage refresh decision.
3
Bidding strategy, Maximize Conversions vs Target CPA
Weeks 5–8 · Google

Once Sofia knows the cost-per-quote baseline (end of Audit), we test switching the top non-brand campaign from Maximize Conversions to Target CPA at a value 10% above baseline. Standard Google Ads experiment, 50/50 traffic split.

Hypothesis · Target CPA lifts efficiency ≥ 15% at flat or higher volume. Significance · 90%, 4 weeks minimum Outcome · Defines bidding strategy for Phase 03 scale.
4
LinkedIn audience, Job-title-led vs Industry-led targeting
Weeks 4–6 · LinkedIn pilot

For Alex Ops, two audience definitions are plausible: (a) Job titles regardless of industry, (b) Industry-led with broader title list. Cost and quality may differ meaningfully. Run two audiences, ~$1.5K each, measure CPL and quality.

Hypothesis · Industry-led wins on cost; title-led wins on quality. Significance · 90%, ≥50 leads per arm Outcome · Defines LinkedIn targeting approach for scale.
5
Geo bid adjustment, Urban metros vs rural Canada
Weeks 6–9 · Google

Audit will likely show GTA, Montreal, Vancouver Metro converting at much higher rates than rural geographies. Test bid adjustments of +30% for top metros and –50% for the lowest-converting regions. Standard A/B with the geo bid modifier as the variable.

Hypothesis · Geo bid tuning lifts overall efficiency ≥ 10%. Significance · 90%, 4 weeks minimum Outcome · Locked into Phase 03 scale plan.
⚠ The discipline that makes this work

One test per channel at a time. Tests are pre-registered: hypothesis, sample size, significance threshold, and decision rule written down before the test starts. Results documented even when they're inconclusive. The point of the testing agenda isn't to win every test, it's to stop guessing.
