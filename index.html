The strategic thesis

We don't have a demand problem.
We have a conversion problem.

People are already reaching FreightSimple — through ads, direct visits, referrals. The leak isn't at the top of the funnel; it's at every stage after. 68% bounce on the homepage. 100+ companies quoted and never booked. 90+ booked once and never returned. Investing in more traffic while the funnel leaks is filling a broken bucket. Q2 is about fixing the bucket. Q3 we scale demand — on top of a funnel that actually holds.

Sales shadowing deepened this — see Strategy Base · /03 for how the thesis expands with qualitative data from the front line.

/03Strategy base · the qualitative layer

What sales shadowing taught us.

The audit gave us numbers. A shadowing session with the sales team gave us context. Combining both reveals a sharper picture of what we're actually fighting — and how to respond. This section captures that thinking, and maps it to what changes across every deliverable.

✦
Source · Sales shadowing · April 23, 2026 Our clients aren't loyal to us. They're loyal to the lowest quote. Sales confirmed what the funnel data was already whispering: customers split shipments across platforms based on price-per-shipment. Each quote is a fresh competition against Freightera, Freightcom, and carrier-direct. That reframes the entire problem — retention isn't "get them to book twice." It's "earn every single shipment, every time."
Decision driver ● High impact

Price is the #1 decision factor — every time.

Clients split shipments across platforms based on who offers the best price for that specific shipment. Freightera one day, us the next. They rarely see the value-add — they see the quote.
What this means Value-add features (bill auditing, anomaly detection, 100+ carriers) aren't being translated to dollars. We need to reframe messaging: not "what we do," but "what it saves you per shipment."
Audience profile Confirms ICP

Decision-makers are corporate, time-starved, serious.

They speak fast. They're serious. They value human contact but want it concise. No fluff. No long sales conversations. Quick, trust-building, done.
What this means Validates Microsoft Edge signal (24.89% traffic = corporate fingerprint). Copy across the site must be tight, substantive, no fluff. Emails must be short. Long-form content gets skipped.
Automation gap ● Blocks scale

Everything runs on manual CRM templates.

There's no automated email flow. Follow-ups, surveys, re-engagements — all sent manually from the CRM, template by template. Which means most accounts never get one. Hundreds of quoted clients sit in silence.
What this means Retention Sequence becomes critical path, not "nice to have." Klaviyo setup is the highest-leverage ops move we can make. One person in sales cannot manually nurture hundreds of accounts.
Relationship priority ● Strategic core

Strengthening relationships is the #1 internal priority.

Large, frequent shippers are the focus — as they should be. But there's a long tail of mid-volume accounts silently drifting. With one person handling all of sales, most don't get real attention. We need automation to cover them.
What this means Beyond retention, we need a dormant account reactivation play (already in Sprint 02) plus ongoing low-touch nurture. Goal: every account feels seen, even the small ones.
Supply-side opportunity Marketing lever

No carrier scoring exists — but it should.

We've considered carrier scoring and decided it wouldn't add value. Marketing sees it differently — a scoring system would let us curate the carrier network, offer better placement to top performers, and build a defensible story ("our carriers average 97% on-time").
What this means Add Carrier Scoring to Q3 pipeline as a cross-functional initiative (Ops + Marketing). Turns an operational metric into a marketplace differentiator and a marketing narrative.
Positioning clarity Message fix

Clients want parcel + freight. We only do LTL.

Prospects ask about parcel all the time. It's a completely different business. We need to be explicit and unambiguous: FreightSimple is LTL-only — not because we can't, but because specialization is the value.
What this means Homepage and nav need to clarify what we do and don't do, upfront. Turn the limitation into a positioning win: "The LTL specialists for Canadian shippers." Become the category authority.

/04SEO deep-dive · the hidden audit

Not absent. Saturated with content that doesn't work.

The site was assumed to have no SEO. A closer look revealed the opposite: 179 indexed pages across four country/language variants, most of them programmatically generated (Book Saia LTL · Book Manitoulin LTL · Yukon LTL Freight Experts, and so on). And yet: zero organic traffic, zero ranking keywords. This changes the entire SEO play — cleanup and consolidation matter more than creation.

179/0

Indexed pages vs. monthly organic visitors

The site is fully indexed by Google — 179 pages across CA/EN (150), US/EN (15), CA/FR (13), NZ/EN (1). Monthly organic traffic: zero. Zero keywords ranking. The content exists but isn't serving the business. That's a very different problem than "no SEO."

Critical findings
Programmatic bloat High severity

150+ templated pages targeting carrier × province

Pages like "Book FedEx LTL", "Book Manitoulin LTL", "Yukon LTL Freight Experts", "Alberta LTL Freight Experts" follow a clear programmatic template. This pattern is exactly what Google's recent updates penalize as "unhelpful, duplicative content at scale."

Traffic: 0 organic/mo
Rankings: 0 keywords
Risk: thin-content signal
Meta titles too short High severity

Core pages are under-optimized on titles

Title tags on key pages sit at 23–29 characters. Google's recommended range is 50–60 — short titles waste prime real estate in search results and fail to set expectations. Every audited page (except homepage) is below threshold.

Pricing: 23 chars
Case Studies / How It Works: 28 chars
Core Features: 29 chars
Heading hierarchy broken Medium severity

Homepage has 50 H3s and 11 H2s

Fifty H3 tags on a single page signals either over-structured content or misused heading tags — Google struggles to understand hierarchy and likely discounts thematic focus. Needs a heading-level audit: what should actually be H2 vs H3 vs regular paragraphs.

Homepage: H1:1 / H2:11 / H3:50
Core Features: H1:1 / H2:13 / H3:8
Pricing: H1:1 / H2:5 / H3:32
Duplicate meta keywords Medium severity

Every page uses the same 8 keywords

Identical meta keyword block on every page audited: "LTL freight, freight shipping, freight quotes, less than truckload, shipping platform, freight management, cross-border shipping, Canada USA freight." Google hasn't used meta keywords for ranking since 2009, but the repetition also signals a lack of per-page optimization logic.

Per-page uniqueness: none
Targeting logic: absent
Missing meta descriptions High severity

Short or missing descriptions on core pages

The meta description is the snippet shown in search results — it's the pitch that earns the click. Case Studies page runs 109 characters (below the 150–160 recommended); several others we haven't audited yet likely have the same issue or missing descriptions entirely.

◆ The strategic reframe

Cleanup and consolidation before new content creation.

The original read was "FreightSimple has zero SEO presence — build content from scratch." The real picture is different: FreightSimple has a 179-page SEO footprint that isn't working. Before investing in new content, the existing footprint needs a decision — keep, consolidate, or deprecate — and the core pages need on-page fixes (titles, descriptions, heading hierarchy).

This is actually a faster path to organic growth than starting from zero. The domain is already indexed. The backlink foundation exists. Fix what's there first; then add new, intentional content that targets queries with real search volume.
